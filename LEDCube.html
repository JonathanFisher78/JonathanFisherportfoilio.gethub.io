LED Cube

The Project

I saw an LED cube on YouTube a few years ago, and it’s been in the back of my mind to make one ever since. It seemed simple at first (I was wrong!) but really cool. The most amazing part was seeing one run on an Arduino controlling hundreds of LEDs individually even though it had less than 30 outputs. My goals were to learn PCB design, how to use shift registers, improve my soldering skills, and learn how to code a multiplexing circuit. I followed a tutorial on GitHub for the frame and used ChatGPT to help with my circuit design and programming.

The Proposal

The idea was to make a 6x6x6 cube—big enough for cool effects while not so large that it would be impossible to repair LEDs buried deep inside. I laser-cut a frame to help solder the LEDs and built a test circuit with shift registers on a 7-segment display as proof of concept for controlling more outputs than the Arduino natively supports.

The Process

I soldered each of the six layers in the laser-cut jig, then stacked them into the full cube. Each layer took about an hour, and combining them was even longer—for a total of about 15 hours of soldering. The wiring works by connecting all 36 LEDs of a layer to a common negative lead, while each vertical column shares a positive lead. That way, a single LED can be selected by powering one layer and one column. I designed the circuit in KiCAD and ordered a PCB. After troubleshooting a transistor wiring mistake and patching it, I finally had a working board.

The Product

The final result is a 3D LED display that runs custom effects and graphics. Multiplexing makes it appear as though all 216 LEDs are on at once by cycling quickly through each layer. I also 3D printed a box to act as a stand and to house the electronics. This project was both fun and challenging—and it served as my master project in the Invention Studio to become certified on electronics tools. The cube is currently on display in Bobby Dodd Stadium as part of the ME Student Wing project showcase.

The Possible

If I were to redo this project, I’d want to build a larger 8x8x8 cube for higher resolution, and possibly use RGB LEDs to add full color effects.
